Study Summary: Modeling Loudness Nonlinearity

Paper: Considerations in modeling the nonlinearity of human hearing for loudness perception
Authors: Becker, Sottek, Liu, & Bender (2025)

The Core Question

How accurately do modern standardized loudness models (ISO 532-1, ISO 532-3, and ECMA 418-2) model the fundamental **nonlinear relationship** between physical sound pressure level (SPL) and perceived loudness? The paper seeks to prove that simplifying this function introduces significant errors into traditional models.

1. Methodology and Historical Context

A. The Problem of Nonlinearity

Human hearing compresses sound levels nonlinearly. The historical **Power Law** simplified this, stating that above 40 phon, every increase of **10 phon doubles the perceived loudness in sone** (the "10 dB rule"). Traditional ISO standards (532-1 and 532-3) still rely on this simplified rule, with minor modifications for low levels.

B. Models Compared

  1. ISO 532-1 (Zwicker Method): Uses the simplified power law, leading to a consistent, smooth curve.
  2. ISO 532-3 (Moore-Glasberg-Schlittenlacher Method): Uses a relationship table similar to the power law.
  3. ECMA 418-2 (Sottek Hearing Model): Uses a **piecewise-defined exponents** function, dividing the hearing range into eight segments, allowing the slope to change continuously and accurately.

C. Protocol for Evaluation

  • Reference Data: The models were compared against **Zwicker's original experimental data** from 1958 on "double and half loudness perception." This historical data represents the human ground truth.
  • Metric: The conversion curve from Loudness Level (phon) to Loudness (sone) generated by each model was plotted against the experimental data corridor (the mean, upper, and lower quartiles).

2. Key Findings and Conclusion

A. Failure of the Simplified Power Law

The models using the simplified power law (ISO 532-1 and ISO 532-3) consistently showed a deviation from the experimental mean:

  • For levels **below 40 phon**, they **underestimated** loudness.
  • For levels **above 40 phon**, they **overestimated** loudness (e.g., at 80 phon, the model predicted 16 sone, while the experimental mean suggested 9.8 sone).

B. Superiority of ECMA 418-2

The ECMA 418-2 standard's piecewise, segmented approach to nonlinearity resulted in a mapping that:

  • Fits Zwicker's experimental mean curve **much more closely** across the entire 10-80 phon range.
  • Consistently falls within the narrow **experimental corridor** defined by the upper and lower quartiles of the original human listening data.

C. Conclusion on Loudness Unit

The paper concludes that the choice of a more precise mathematical function for the fundamental phon-to-sone mapping is critical. This difference results in the ECMA 418-2 model using a unique unit ($sone_{HMS}$) that is not directly comparable to the classical Sone unit used by the ISO standards, underscoring the deep structural differences between the models.

3. Jargon & Technical Terminology Buster

Nonlinearity of Hearing
The fact that the perceived increase in loudness does not perfectly match the physical increase in sound energy. The ear compresses loud sounds differently than soft sounds.
Phon
The unit of Loudness Level. It references a physical sound pressure level (SPL).
Sone
The unit of Loudness Magnitude. It represents how *loud* a sound feels. It is the output of the nonlinear transformation from phon.
Power Law
The simplified historical math model that states loudness grows exponentially with intensity (often interpreted as the "10 dB doubles loudness" rule). It is the basis for ISO 532-1.
Piecewise-Defined Exponents
A complex mathematical function used by ECMA 418-2 where the transformation curve is broken into several small segments (8 segments in this model), allowing the slope to change dynamically to match precise human hearing data.
