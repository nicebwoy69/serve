Study Summary: Modeling the ISO 226:2023 Loudness Contours

Paper: Modeling the ISO 226:2023 Equal-Loudness-Level Contours by Standardized Loudness Methods
Authors: Sottek, Lobato, Bender, & Becker (2023)

The Core Question

1. Methodology and Test Protocol

A. The Reference Standard (ISO 226:2023)

The **ISO 226:2023** curves are the reference target. These curves define the sound pressure level (in dB) required for pure continuous tones of different frequencies to be perceived as having the same loudness level (in phon) by human listeners. The 2023 version contains minor corrections and clarifications over the 2003 version, and these contours serve as the gold standard for validating loudness models.

B. Models Tested

The study tested three major psychoacoustic loudness methods:

  1. ISO 532-1:2017 (Zwicker Method): Based on the older ISO 226:1987 data. Uses a third-octave filter bank.
  2. ISO 532-3:2023 (Moore-Glasberg-Schlittenlacher Method): The updated standard for time-varying loudness. It aims to match the ISO 226:2003 data.
  3. ECMA 418-2 2nd Edition (Sottek Hearing Model): A newer standardized method that models sub-critical bandwidth and tonal/noise loudness components.

C. Protocol for Model Evaluation

The models were evaluated for 20, 40, 60, and 80 phon levels across frequencies from 100 Hz to 10 kHz.

  • Matching Procedure: For each model, the authors calculated the resulting **sone value** (the loudness unit) at 1 kHz for a given phon level. They then determined the necessary dB level for every other frequency required to yield that **same sone value** according to the model's calculation.
  • Metric: The accuracy of the model-generated contours was quantified using the **Root-Mean-Squared (RMS) difference** in dB between the model's prediction and the official ISO 226:2023 reference table.

2. Results and Model Comparison

The table below summarizes how accurately each model replicated the human perception data defined by ISO 226:2023:

Loudness Method RMS Difference [dB] (Lower is Better) Notes on Performance
ECMA 418-2 (Sottek Model) 1.57 dB **Best Match.** Shows superior accuracy, especially in the critical mid-frequency range (1 kHz to 5 kHz), where other models overestimate loudness.
ISO 532-3 (Moore-Glasberg-Schlittenlacher) 3.04 dB Reproduces the curves with reasonable accuracy. One order of magnitude slower calculation time than ISO 532-1.
ISO 532-1 (Zwicker Method) 5.55 dB **Poorest Match.** Its failure is expected, as it was designed to match the much older, substantially different ISO 226:1987 contours. Also prone to **fluctuations** due to steep filter characteristics.

A. Calculation Efficiency (Computational Cost)

The methods were also benchmarked for speed:

  • ISO 532-1 was the fastest (baseline factor of 1).
  • ECMA 418-2 was 6 times slower (due to separating tonal and noise components).
  • ISO 532-3 was 22 times slower than ISO 532-1, making its application to long, complex signals "cumbersome."

B. Conclusion on Model Dominance

The authors conclude that the **ECMA 418-2 (Sottek Loudness)** has established itself as the dominant model in terms of result quality, demonstrating equal or superior performance across pure tones, sub-critical bandwidth noise, and multi-tone components.

ISO 226:2023
The international standard defining the **Equal-Loudness-Level Contours**â€”the fundamental curves showing what sound pressure level (in dB) is needed at different frequencies to sound equally loud to a human ear.
Phon
The unit of loudness level. It defines the perceived loudness of a sound relative to a 1 kHz tone.
Sone
The unit of actual loudness magnitude (how much louder one sound is than another). 1 Sone = 40 phon.
RMS Difference
**Root-Mean-Squared Difference.** The main metric used for model comparison. It measures the average magnitude of the error (in dB) between the model's prediction and the true human perception data. A lower RMS difference means the model is more accurate.
Sub-Critical Bandwidth Noise
A specialized noise signal whose frequency bandwidth is narrower than the ear's critical filter (or critical band). Predicting the loudness of these signals is difficult because the energy is highly concentrated.
