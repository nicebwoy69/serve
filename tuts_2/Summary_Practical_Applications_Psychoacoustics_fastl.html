Study Summary: Practical Applications of Psychoacoustics

Paper: Praktische Anwendungen der Psychoakustik (Practical Applications of Psychoacoustics)
Author: Hugo Fastl (2010)

The Core Principle: The Psychoacoustic Link

Psychoacoustics acts as the essential quantitative link between the objective **World of Physical Measurement** (Frequency, Level) and the subjective **World of Auditory Sensation** (Pitch, Loudness, Roughness).

The paper stresses the critical need for a clear distinction between the physical *stimulus* and the perceived *sensation*.

1. The Loudness Problem (Relevance to Your Project)

A. Loudness vs. Level

The common, casual errorâ€”even among professionalsâ€”is confusing **Level (dB)**, a physical metric, with **Loudness (Lautheit)**, a sensation. Correctly, a 10 dB reduction in level typically results in a reduction of loudness by half. Measuring this nonlinear relationship is the foundation of all objective psychoacoustic modeling.

B. Contextual & Cognitive Effects

Perception is highly contextual, not just acoustic:

  • Pitch Shift: The perceived pitch of a fixed-frequency tone can increase significantly (up to a musical semitone) when a low-frequency noise is added (see image below for measured example). This proves that the brain uses context to build sensation.
  • Cognitive Aversion: The perceived annoyance of a noise type (e.g., motorcycle sound) may remain high even if the loudness is significantly reduced, because the listener's **aversion** to the source overrides the acoustic properties.
  • Audiovisual Bias: **Red** and **dark green** colored objects (like trains or sports cars) are perceived as **significantly louder** than light-colored objects (like light green or blue) when the physical sound level is identical.

2. Applications and Findings

A. Music and Selective Hearing

  • Harmonicity/Inharmonicity: Composers like Pythagoras performed the first psychoacoustic experiments on consonance. More recently, studies showed that sounds from electric guitars are slightly **inharmonic** and that musicians often prefer this "physical flaw."
  • Selective Filtering: The sound of a large bell contains 30 partial tones, but listeners only need 4 to 12 of them to perceive a "good bell sound." The brain filters the sound to extract only the most important perceptual components.

B. Noise Assessment and Model Validation

  • Noise Reduction Metrics: When evaluating new sound-absorbing road surfaces, the standard rule of thumb ("10 dB less is half as loud") significantly *underestimated* the perceived noise reduction compared to measurements using psychoacoustic models (like the Loudness N5 percentile). This validates the need for models in complex noise assessment.
  • Vehicle Audibility: For quiet vehicles (Hybrid/Electric), predicting the "critical distance" at which a pedestrian can hear the vehicle in background noise requires sophisticated psychoacoustic masking models, as simple SPL metrics are insufficient for collision avoidance.
