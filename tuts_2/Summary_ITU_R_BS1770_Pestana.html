Study Summary: ITU-R BS.1770 Modification for Multitrack Audio

Paper: Loudness Measurement of Multitrack Audio Content Using Modifications of ITU-R BS.1770
Authors: Pestana & Reiss (2013)

The Core Question

Does the standard ITU-R BS.1770 algorithm (used widely for broadcast mixes) accurately measure the loudness of **individual tracks** within a multitrack context? The study hypothesizes that the algorithm fails for narrow-band, high-frequency material and proposes minimal parameter modifications to improve accuracy.

1. Methodology and Test Protocol (Subjective Evaluation)

A. Loudness Matching Task

  • Subjects: 40 individuals (including professional engineers and audio students).
  • Stimuli: Tracks (stems) from five different songs (9â€“11 tracks per song).
  • Baseline Condition: All tracks were initially normalized to the same loudness value according to the standard ITU-R BS.1770 algorithm (0 LU).
  • Protocol: Subjects were given a **fixed reference track** (either Kick drum or Vocals) and used faders to adjust the level of the remaining tracks until they sounded **equally loud**.

B. Measurement and Analysis

  • Dependent Variable: **Loudness Difference (D)** in Loudness Units (LU). This was the difference between the subject's required level (Evaluation, E) and the algorithm's predicted level (Measurement, M).

    D = Evaluation - Measurement

  • Statistical Testing: Used the **Wilcoxon test** to confirm that the mean differences were statistically significant (not due to random chance), despite high inter-subject variance.

2. Key Findings: Failures of the Standard

A. Systemic Disagreement with Perception

The standard ITU-R algorithm showed major, consistent disagreements with subjective listening:

  • Reference Bias: The mean errors differed significantly depending on whether the Kick Drum or the Vocals were used as the reference track.
  • Overall Error: Mean errors ranged from **-2 LU** (Vocals) to over **-8 LU** (Piano, sampled beat) relative to the Kick Drum reference. This means the standard grossly underestimated the perceived loudness of certain tracks.

B. Correlation with Spectral Content

A correlation analysis identified the primary cause of the algorithmâ€™s failure:

The error in prediction was most strongly correlated with the **$log_{2}$ Spectral Centroid** (a measure of brightness). The relationship showed that the higher the spectral centroid (the brighter/sharper the sound), the more the algorithm underestimated the trackâ€™s true perceived loudness.

This suggests the standard ITU-R BS.1770 high-frequency weighting (Pre-filter and RLB-filter) is not aggressive enough for narrow-band, bright content.

3. Proposed Algorithm Modifications and Validation

A. Parameter Tweaks

The authors analyzed the effects of minimally modifying two parameters within the ITU algorithm:

  • Pre-filter Gain: Varied the high-shelf boost (standard is +4 dB) to increase high-end sensitivity.
  • Time Constant: Varied the integration window (standard is 400 ms) to see how temporal processing affected the results.

B. Optimal Result

Four optimization scenarios were tested, revealing that the most appropriate modifications always involved a **higher Pre-filter Gain** than the standard +4 dB.

  • Scenario Alpha (Minimal Error): The best parameters were found to be a **280 ms Time Constant** and a **+10 dB Pre-filter Gain**. This combination improved accuracy by 14% over the standard.

C. Expert Validation

A small follow-up test (MUSHRA-style) with three professional mixing engineers validated the findings. The resulting mixes using the **280 ms / +10 dB** parameters scored significantly higher (better equal-loudness achievement) than the mixes produced using the standard ITU/EBU recommendation.

ITU-R BS.1770 / EBU R128
Industry standards for measuring audio program loudness (the algorithm that yields LUFS). They combine frequency weighting (K-weighting) and energy integration.
Spectral Centroid
A metric that represents the "center of mass" of a sound's spectrum. A higher centroid means the sound is perceived as brighter, sharper, or thinner (e.g., a hi-hat).
Pre-filter Gain
The amount of boost (gain) applied by the first filter in the ITU algorithm, which specifically emphasizes high frequencies. The standard uses +4 dB.
Time Constant / Windowing
The time duration over which the average signal level is measured. This affects how the meter responds to quick, short-term changes.
Loudness Unit (LU)
A logarithmic unit of loudness used by ITU and EBU standards. 1 LU is equivalent to 1 dB.
