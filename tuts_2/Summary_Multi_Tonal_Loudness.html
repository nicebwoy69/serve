Study Summary: Modeling Perceived Tonal Loudness

Paper: Modeling the perceived tonal loudness of multiple tonal components
Authors: Lobato & Sottek (2023)

The Core Question

How does the loudness of complex tones (multiple harmonics or partials) change when a single component is altered? This study tests the accuracy of the Sottek Hearing Model's Tonal Loudness component against empirical data from listening tests involving multi-tone signals.

1. Methodology and Test Protocol

A. Models and Structure

The core model under investigation is the **Sottek Hearing Model (SHM)**, standardized in ECMA 418-2, which separates sound into tonal and noise components. The calculation steps involve filtering, auditory bank analysis, and specific weighing of the separated components.

B. Listening Test Design

  • Subjects: 16 participants with self-reported normal hearing.
  • Task: Loudness Matching (Method of Adjustment). Subjects adjusted a slider to make a modified complex tone sound equally loud as the original reference tone.
  • Stimuli: Harmonic complex tones with fundamental frequencies (f0) of 700 Hz and 1000 Hz. The number of harmonics was varied (2 or 4).
  • Key Manipulation: The amplitude of only **one harmonic** was changed (e.g., multiplied by a factor of 3x or 9x, corresponding to a level increase of 9.5 dB or 19 dB).

C. Statistical Methods

  • Analysis: Measured the **Level Difference at Equally Perceived Loudness** (dB).
  • Bayesian Framework: Applied **Bayes' rule** to the results to increase robustness, calculating the probability of the *parameters given the experimental data* ($P(\mu,\sigma|E)$), which is more reliable for small sample sizes than traditional statistics.
  • Metric: Calculated the **Mean Absolute Error (MAE)** to compare the error of the three models.

2. Key Findings and Model Comparison

The study compared the Sottek Hearing Model's tonal loudness calculation (Tonal Loudness) against the overall loudness prediction of the older ISO 532-1 and ISO 532-3 standards.

A. Model Agreement on Tonal Loudness

For the core tests (multi-tone signals with altered amplitude), the predictions of all three models fell largely within the 95% credible interval of the listening experiment. This suggests that all three models can generally handle the spectral summation effects of multi-tone complexes well.

B. Superior Accuracy of SHM

When evaluated using the Mean Absolute Error (MAE), the models ranked as follows (lower error is better):

Method Mean Absolute Error [dB]
Sottek Hearing Model 0.60 dB
ISO 532-3 0.69 dB
ISO 532-1 0.77 dB

The Sottek Hearing Model (ECMA 418-2) showed the lowest error, making it the most accurate predictor for these multi-tone signals.

C. Tonality Metric Shortcoming

The paper noted that while **SHM Tonal Loudness** is excellent, the older **SHM Tonality** *metric* (which estimates the prominence of a tone, not its loudness) fails for signals with multiple prominent components because it was designed only for a single dominant tone.

D. Conclusion

The Tonal Loudness component of the Sottek Hearing Model accurately predicts the perceived loudness of complex multi-tone signals and is slightly superior to the current ISO standards. This confirms the model's strength in handling signals with complex spectral content.

3. Jargon & Technical Terminology Buster

Tonal Loudness
The perceived loudness contributed specifically by the pure tone components (harmonics, partials) of a sound, separate from the surrounding noise.
Harmonic Complex Tone
A sound composed of a fundamental frequency (f0) and integer multiples (harmonics) of that frequency.
Bayesian Framework / Prior
A statistical approach used to analyze the listening data. A "prior" is initial knowledge (e.g., that loudness is usually based on signal energy) that helps make the experimental results more robust, especially with smaller groups of participants.
Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
A statistical metric used to compare model accuracy. It measures the average magnitude of the difference (error) between the model's prediction and the real human judgment. A lower MAE means higher accuracy.
95% Credible Interval
The range around the experimental mean where the true average result is expected to fall 95% of the time, according to Bayesian statistics.
