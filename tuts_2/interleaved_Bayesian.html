Feasibility of Interleaved Bayesian Adaptive Procedures in Estimating the Equal-Loudness Contour

Abstract

A Bayesian adaptive procedure, the interleaved-equal-loudness contour (IELC) procedure, was developed to improve the efficiency in estimating the equal-loudness contour. Experiment 1 evaluated the test-retest reliability of the IELC procedure using six naive normal-hearing listeners. Two IELC runs of 200 trials were conducted and excellent test-retest reliability was found at both the group and individual levels. Using the same group of listeners, Experiment 2 compared the IELC procedure to two other procedures that required frequency-by-frequency testing: the commonly adopted interleaved staircase (ISC) procedure and the interleaved maximum-likelihood (IML) procedure. The accuracies of all three procedures measured in Experiment 2 were similar, which was superior to the accuracies of the initial IELC runs, indicating a potential training effect. The study concludes that the IELC procedure demonstrates satisfactory efficiency and reliability for estimating equal-loudness contours.

I. Introduction and Background

Loudness quantification is often difficult and time-consuming. A common method involves finding the point of subjective equality (PSE)â€”the level of a test stimulus required to match the loudness of a standard stimulus. PSE measurements are sensitive to biases from stimulus range and sequential order.

Jargon Explained: Equal-Loudness Contour
This is a curve on a graph that shows the sound pressure levels (in dB SPL) across a range of frequencies (like $250\text{ Hz}$ to $4000\text{ Hz}$) that are all perceived as having the same loudness level as a fixed reference tone. Estimating this curve efficiently is the primary goal of the paper.

The Need for Efficiency and the Adaptive Procedures

Traditionally, equal-loudness contours are measured frequency-by-frequency, which is inefficient. The study proposes the Interleaved Equal-Loudness Contour (IELC) procedure to estimate the entire contour simultaneously, taking advantage of the assumed correlation (or smoothness) among PSEs in adjacent frequency regions.

The IELC procedure is compared against two predecessors that estimate PSE one frequency at a time:

  • ISC (Interleaved Staircase) Procedure: Proposed by Jesteadt (1980), this procedure simultaneously measures two adaptive tracks: a high-probability track (e.g., $71\%$ "louder" responses) and a low-probability track (e.g., $29\%$ "louder" responses). The PSE is the average of the stimulus levels at these two points on the psychometric function.
  • IML (Interleaved Maximum-Likelihood) Procedure: This is a Bayesian adaptive procedure (a modification of the Updated Maximum-Likelihood, UML, procedure) which iteratively optimizes stimulus choice based on previous responses to estimate the PSE one frequency at a time. It also uses two interleaved tracks for high and low response probabilities.

II. The Interleaved Equal-Loudness Contour (IELC) Procedure

A. Overview and Computational Steps

The IELC procedure estimates two tracks simultaneously: a high-probability contour (e.g., $75\%$ response probability) and a low-probability contour (e.g., $25\%$ response probability). The PSE contour is the average of these two contours.

The core innovation is modeling the entire equal-loudness contour as a piece-wise cubic interpolation curve based on a small number of anchor points (five in this study). This model assumes covariance across adjacent frequencies, meaning data collected at one frequency informs the estimate at nearby frequencies, which significantly speeds up testing.

Following each trial, the procedure uses the response to update:

  1. The likelihood of all $M=462$ candidate models via Extended Kalman Filtering (a technique to treat noisy observations from a dynamical system).
  2. The model with the maximum-likelihood provides the current best estimate of the contour (the "Interim Model").
  3. This Interim Model is used to select the optimal stimulus level for the next trial, ensuring stimuli are sampled near the estimated high- or low-probability contours.

B. Psychometric Functions (Link Functions)

The procedure uses logistic functions as link functions between the stimulus level ($L$) and the probability of a "louder" response ($p$). These functions define the dynamic range of the psychometric function.

High-Probability Track (75% Threshold)

The probability of a "louder" response ($p_{H,m}$) for the $m^{\text{th}}$ candidate model is:

$$p_{H,m}[f,L;\Theta_{H,m}(f;\phi_{H,m,i}),\beta] = 0.5 + 0.5 \left\{ 1 + e^{-\beta[L - \Theta_{H,m}(f;\phi_{H,m,i})]} \right\}^{-1}$$

Low-Probability Track (25% Threshold)

The probability of a "louder" response ($p_{L,m}$) for the $m^{\text{th}}$ candidate model is:

$$p_{L,m}[f,L;\Theta_{L,m}(f;\phi_{L,m,i}),\beta] = 0.5 \left\{ 1 + e^{-\beta[L - \Theta_{L,m}(f;\phi_{L,m,i})]} \right\}^{-1}$$
Explanation of Parameters and Symbols:
  • $p_{H,m}, p_{L,m}$: The predicted probability of a "louder" response for the high ($H$) or low ($L$) track, based on the $m^{\text{th}}$ candidate model.
  • $f$: The frequency of the test stimulus (e.g., $500\text{ Hz}$). The PSE is estimated as a function of this variable.
  • $L$: The sound pressure level (in $\text{dB SPL}$) of the test stimulus presented in the trial.
  • $\Theta_{H,m}, \Theta_{L,m}$: The predicted threshold contour (high or low probability) from the $m^{\text{th}}$ candidate model. This is the **estimated sound level** that corresponds to the target response probability ($75\%$ or $25\%$) at a given frequency $f$.
  • $\phi_{H,m,i}, \phi_{L,m,i}$: A vector containing the five free parameters (levels of the anchor points) that define the $m^{\text{th}}$ model's threshold contour following the $i^{\text{th}}$ trial.
  • $\beta$: The slope parameter of the logistic psychometric function. A larger value of $\beta$ indicates a steeper slope, meaning listeners are more precise in their judgments (a smaller change in $L$ results in a larger change in response probability). In this study, $\beta=0.25$ was fixed.
  • $e$: The base of the natural logarithm (approximately $2.718$).

In essence, these formulas ensure that if the stimulus level ($L$) is close to the estimated threshold contour ($\Theta$), the response probability is near $75\%$ (or $25\%$).

C. Post Hoc Model Fit

After data collection, the PSE for each test frequency is calculated by fitting a logistic regression model to the collected trial data from both tracks:

$$p(r_{i}=1) = \left[ 1 + e^{-b(L_{i}-\text{PSE})} \right]^{-1}$$

where $r_i=1$ indicates a "louder" response.

Explanation of Post Hoc Symbols:
  • $r_i$: The response collected on the $i^{\text{th}}$ trial ($1$ for "louder," $0$ for "weaker").
  • $b$: The fitted slope parameter (similar to $\beta$, but fitted to the pooled data post-experiment).
  • $\text{PSE}$: The final estimated **Point of Subjective Equality** for a given frequency. This corresponds to the $50\%$ response probability point on the fitted psychometric function.

III. Experiment 1: Test-Retest Reliability of IELC

Methods

  • Subjects: Six naive normal-hearing listeners.
  • Stimuli: A standard $1\text{ kHz}$ pure tone at $60\text{ dB SPL}$ was compared against a test tone (frequency and level determined by IELC). Tone duration was $300\text{ ms}$.
  • Task: Two pure tones were presented sequentially; listeners indicated which tone sounded louder.
  • Procedure: Two IELC runs (Test and Retest) of 200 trials each were conducted. The second run used slightly different prior parameter distributions to test the robustness of convergence.

Results (Experiment 1)

The estimated high- and low-probability contours were consistent across the two $200\text{-trial}$ IELC runs for individual listeners, demonstrating convergence.

The root-mean-square (rms) deviation between the two estimated equal-loudness contours ranged from $3.0\text{ to } 5.5\text{ dB}$ (mean of $4.3\text{ dB}$), indicating excellent test-retest reliability despite the low trial count and lack of prior training. The difference in prior configurations did not significantly affect the reliability, suggesting the procedure is robust.

IV. Experiment 2: Comparison with ISC and IML Procedures

A. General Methods

The same six listeners participated. This experiment included $ISC$ runs, $IML$ runs (approximately $500\text{ trials}$ each), and a third $IELC$ run to test reliability over a longer time interval.

B. Results (Experiment 2)

  • Reliability Comparison: The rms deviation for the ISC ($4.5\text{ dB}$) and IML ($5.2\text{ dB}$) procedures (both using $\sim 500\text{ trials}$) was comparable to the IELC ($4.3\text{ dB}$, using only $200\text{ trials}$). This suggests the IELC procedure offers superior efficiency for a given reliability target.
  • Long-Term Reliability: The PSEs from the first and third IELC runs (tested at least one week apart) were not significantly different, showing the general shape of the contour is stable.
  • Accuracy Comparison: All procedures in Experiment 2 (after listeners were trained) showed similar accuracy ($\text{rms error}$ typically between $3\text{ and } 4\text{ dB}$) when compared against a "best estimate" contour derived from pooling all data. The initial IELC runs in Experiment 1 were less accurate, indicating a potential training effect.

C. Stimulus Sampling Patterns

The procedures sampled the stimulus space differently, reflecting their distinct algorithms:

  • IELC: Stimuli were sampled along the high- ($75\%$) and low- ($25\%$) probability contours, leading to a bimodal distribution of sampled levels just above or below the PSE. This design successfully prevented listeners from being forced to compare two very similar loudness levels ($50\%$ point), which often causes difficulty and bias.
  • ISC & IML: These procedures sampled stimuli most frequently near the PSE itself ($0\text{ dB deviation}$), often leading to a unimodal distribution clustered around the $50\%$ point.

V. General Discussion and Limitations

A. Performance of the IELC Procedure

The IELC procedure successfully estimated the equal-loudness contour across multiple frequencies simultaneously with excellent test-retest reliability using only $200\text{ trials}$. This time efficiency is achieved by modeling the contour using a small number of parameters, assuming the PSEs across adjacent frequencies are correlated.

B. Limitations of the IELC Procedure

The IELC procedure relies on the assumption that the equal-loudness contour is relatively smooth. If the contour exhibits fine, complex structures (known as cochlear fine structure), the simple model used (piece-wise cubic interpolation across five anchor points) may not be sufficient to capture local details.

Monte-Carlo simulations confirmed this limitation:

  • If the "true" contour is highly complex, the accuracy of the IELC estimate decreases.
  • The optimal number of anchor points ($k_{IELC}$) depends on the complexity of the contour being measured. Choosing too few points results in insufficient detail, while choosing too many can lead to overfitting (fitting noise instead of the true contour shape).

C. Individual Differences

Despite the high intra-subject reliability (test-retest), substantial individual differences were observed across the six listeners. The standard deviation across listeners was highest at the lowest and highest test frequencies (up to $10.4\text{ dB}$ at $4000\text{ Hz}$), confirming the known variability of the equal-loudness contour across individuals.

VI. Summary

The IELC procedure is a robust and efficient Bayesian adaptive technique for estimating the equal-loudness contour. With only $200\text{ trials}$, it achieved reliability comparable to the $ISC$ and $IML$ procedures requiring over $500\text{ trials}$. The procedure is recommended for individualized loudness control applications, though further study is needed on the duration and type of initial training required to achieve maximum accuracy.
